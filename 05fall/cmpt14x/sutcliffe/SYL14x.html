COURSE OBJECTIVE
This group of courses is designed to provide an introduction to programming and to a modern high level
language (Modula-2) so that the student becomes a competent programmer. Emphasis is placed on Structured
Programming techniques. By the end of this course the student will be able to define a problem, determine the
necessary input/output requirements, prepare an algorithm to solve the problem, write structured Modula-2
code, debug the program, and produce documentation specifying how the program can be used and the methods
by which the program achieves its objectives.

145: TWU Cmpt 140 or the equivalent ( must be Modula-2)
NOTES
1. Beginning programmers with some previous computing knowledge take CMPT 140, and if they wish to
continue beyond introductory computing, must take CMPT 145 in the second six weeks.
2. Students who have University level credit for a programming course in a structured language such as Pascal,
Ada, or C may already have transfer credit for a first year courseIn this case, the credit for this 14x course is for
CMPT 143-2 (Introduction to a Second Higher Level Language). The latter, as a second course, requires fewer
hours to learn the language, and has reduced assignments.
3. Students who have University level credit for a 3 semester hour programming course in Modula-2 and credit
or transfer credit for CMPT 140 take only CMPT 145 only. Any such students not taking CMPT 140 this semester
will have review lab assignments during the first six weeks, and maybut are not required to join the lectures in
progress before the midterm/140 final (about 7 weeks into the course.) Students planning to take ANY more
computing science courses must take CMPT 145 as well as 140. Enroll now.
4. All three of 140/141/143 and of 141/143/145 are offered by the same instructor at the same time and in the
same classroom except that the 140 and 145 students have an additional lecture each week for introductory
material, extra examples, and more review time. CMPT 141/143 students may attend this session if they wish.
5. This course will require four (141/143/145) or five (140) hours of class time per week and laboratory time of
about four hours per week (likely two for 143) using one of the school computers. There is a lab fee. This course
satisfies the science but NOT the labscience requirement for a degree at Trinity Western University.
6. All labs must be done.If two are missing, there is no credit for the course. Hand in what you have done on
your assigned lab day following the indicated first due date; lates are not permitted. Use any other person's
work without attribution and you get zero; do it twice and there will be no credit for the course. Any such
cases also go into the University's files for future reference.
7. This is a course in the fundamentals of a scientific discipline, NOT about hacking together web sites or the
like. You must have excellent English and the ability to concentrate for long periods of time on problem solving.

COURSE OUTLINE:
Topics from Chapter One and General Themes:
Introduction to problem solving
Computers and algorithms in the problem solving process
Abstractions for representing data and program structures
Modula-2 Specific, by chapter:
I Topics for CMPT 140/ 141/143 students (first 7 weeks)
2. Programs, data, literals/constants/variables (real, whole, character)
3. Basic structural abstractions—IF..THEN, BOOLEANS, WHILE, REPEAT
4. Procedures, function procedures, blocks, scope, and local variables
5. Iterations, enumerations and arrays
6. Applications Programming -- Math, Physics, and strings
7. Modules and libraries—use and design
II TopicsCMPT 141/ 143/145 students (last 5.5 weeks)
8. Data Storage Issues: Machine level and file models
9. Intermediate structured data—sets and records; CASE
10. Intermediate programming: parameters, scope,, local modules, exceptions, termination
11. Intermediate Data Structures and methods, recursion
12. Pointers
